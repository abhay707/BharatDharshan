✦Land of Five Rivers✦

Punjab

Capital: Chandigarh Language: Punjabi Region: North India

The land of five rivers — golden mustard fields stretching to the horizon, the radiant marble of Harmandir Sahib reflecting in its sacred pool, and a cuisine of slow-cooked dals and tandoor-blistered breads that has reshaped the subcontinent's table. Punjab's spirit is in the rhythm of the dhol, the crack of sugarcane at harvest, and the open generosity of its langars where every traveller is fed in equal measure beneath one roof.

✦Culture & Arts✦

The Soul of Punjab

01
Classical Dance

Bhangra

A high-energy harvest dance born in the fields of Majha, set to the percussive crack of the dhol and the leap of dancers in chola and kaintha.

02
Folk Dance · Women

Giddha

Circles of women clapping out boliyan — couplets of love, satire and gossip — passed down generations on village rooftops at dusk.

03
Music · Gharana

Patiala Gharana

A nineteenth-century khayal lineage prized for ornamental tans and emotional weight — Bade Ghulam Ali Khan its most luminous voice.

04
Vocal Form

Tappa

Brisk, rapid-phrasing songs adapted from camel drivers' calls — improvised flights on a short text, demanding extraordinary breath control.

05
Textile Art

Phulkari

Literally "flower-work" — silk floss densely embroidered onto khaddar so the ground cloth disappears beneath gardens of stitch.

06
Handicraft

Bagh & Pinjra

The fully-worked bagh shawl — a bride's heirloom — and the lattice-pinjra geometric variation, both worked from the back without a frame.

07
Script · Language

Gurmukhi

"From the Guru's mouth" — the script standardised by Guru Angad Dev in the sixteenth century to carry Punjabi's sacred and secular voice.

08
Notable Figures

Amrita Pritam

The first major Punjabi woman poet — her elegy Aj Aakhan Waris Shah Nu remains the partition's defining lament.

✦Cuisine of Punjab✦

A Table for Everyone

A

Amritsari Kulcha

Breakfast

A blistered, leavened bread stuffed with spiced potatoes, onions or paneer — slapped to the wall of a clay tandoor and pulled out crisp, served with chickpeas and pickled chillies.

Maida, ghee, yoghurt; potato & onion stuffing tempered with amchur, kasoori methi, anardana and green chilli. Finished with butter and a sprinkle of black salt.
Born in the gullies around Amritsar's Hall Bazaar in the late nineteenth century, the kulcha became the morning ritual of merchants visiting the Golden Temple — eaten standing up, washed down with sweet lassi.
M

Makki di Roti & Sarson da Saag

Lunch

The winter signature: a rough cornmeal roti torn in coarse pieces and dipped in slow-cooked mustard greens spiked with ginger and finished with white butter and gud.

Yellow cornmeal patted by hand; mustard, bathua and spinach greens, slow-cooked four hours with garlic, green chillies and a little maize flour. Crowned with makhan and a drizzle of jaggery syrup.
A peasant's banquet — sown in autumn, eaten through the foggy winter months when the Punjab fields turn yellow with mustard. The dish became a national symbol after partition carried it into kitchens across India.
D

Dal Makhani

Dinner

Whole black urad and red kidney beans simmered overnight on dying coals, finished with cream, butter and a long dum — the dish that turned Punjabi cooking into a global cuisine.

Sabut urad, rajma, tomatoes, ginger-garlic, charred green chillies; finished with white butter, cream and a tadka of cumin and Kashmiri chilli. Cooked on slow flame for not less than eight hours.
Devised at Moti Mahal in 1950s Daryaganj by Kundan Lal Gujral — a dish born from refusing to throw away the day's leftover dal, slow-cooked with butter and cream until it became something else entirely.
✦Traditions & Rites✦

Rhythms of the Year

Lohri

Harvest Rite

Lohri — The Bonfire of January

On the longest night of the Punjabi winter, families gather around bonfires to throw in handfuls of til, gud, peanuts and popped corn — offerings to the dying year and to the sun's slow return northward.

Significance
Marks the winter solstice, the end of the rabi sowing, and welcomes the lengthening days. Songs of Dulla Bhatti — the legendary Robin Hood of Punjab — are sung around the flames.

Vaisakhi

Festival · Harvest

Vaisakhi — Birth of the Khalsa

The April festival that crowns the wheat harvest — and the day in 1699 when Guru Gobind Singh founded the Khalsa at Anandpur Sahib. Bhangra circles in the village; nagar kirtans through the city.

Significance
A double commemoration — agrarian thanksgiving and the founding of the Sikh order — that turns every gurdwara into a centre of music, langar and procession.
