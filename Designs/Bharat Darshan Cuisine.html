Punjab

Amritsari Kulcha

Thick leavened bread baked in a tandoor, stuffed with spiced mashed potato and crisped in pure ghee.

Ingredients+
Maida flour, boiled potato, cumin, coriander, green chilli, kasuri methi, ghee, fresh coriander, tandoor coal.
Origin Story+
Born in the bustling bazaars of nineteenth-century Amritsar, the kulcha was the breakfast of merchants, refugees and pilgrims walking to the Golden Temple — a parcel of carbohydrate, butter and warmth that would last till the noon meal.

Punjab

Makki di Roti & Sarson da Saag

The soul of a Punjabi winter — maize flatbread served with slow-cooked mustard greens and a knob of white butter.

Ingredients+
Maize flour, mustard greens, spinach, bathua, ginger, garlic, green chilli, ghee, makkhan, jaggery.
Origin Story+
A dish of the rural rabi harvest — cooked over wood fires in courtyards from December onwards, when the mustard fields turn the Doab a vivid yellow and the Sutlej is at her clearest.

Punjab

Dal Makhani

Black lentils simmered overnight with butter and cream until the gravy becomes silky, almost meaty in texture.

Ingredients+
Whole urad dal, rajma, tomato, ginger-garlic, garam masala, butter, cream, kasuri methi.
Origin Story+
Codified in mid-twentieth-century Delhi by Kundan Lal Gujral, who needed a vegetarian counterpart to his tandoori chicken at Moti Mahal — a slow-simmer recipe that has since travelled to every corner of the world.

SLunch
Kerala

Sadya

A 26-dish feast served on a fresh banana leaf — the ultimate expression of Kerala hospitality and ritual order.

Ingredients+
Rice, sambar, parippu, avial, thoran, olan, kalan, pachadi, kichadi, papadom, banana, payasam, coconut, curd.
Origin Story+
The Sadya is the meal of Onam — King Mahabali's annual return to a Kerala that, legend holds, knew no inequality. Each dish is placed in a precise position on the leaf; the eating is itself a memory of paradise.

MDinner
Kerala

Meen Curry

Fish in a tangy, fiery coconut-tamarind gravy — cooked slow in a clay chatti until the spice has gone into the bone.

Ingredients+
Kingfish or pearl spot, kudampuli (Malabar tamarind), red chilli, shallot, ginger, curry leaf, coconut oil, fenugreek.
Origin Story+
A dish of the Malabar fishing villages, where the catch comes in at dawn and is cooking by mid-morning. The clay pot lends an earthy edge no metal vessel can replicate; the curry tastes better on the second day.

PBreakfast
Kerala

Puttu & Kadala Curry

Steamed cylinders of rice flour layered with grated coconut, served with a spiced black-chickpea curry — Kerala's beloved dawn meal.

Ingredients+
Rice flour, fresh coconut, salt, water, black chickpea, shallot, mustard seed, curry leaf, coconut oil.
Origin Story+
The puttu kutti — a bamboo-tube steamer — is among the oldest cooking implements in southern India. Generations of Malayalis have woken to the hiss of this device before the first rooster.

DLunch
Rajasthan

Dal Baati Churma

Baked wheat balls split and drowned in five-lentil dal, served with a sweet crumble of churma rich in ghee and jaggery.

Ingredients+
Whole wheat, semolina, panchmel dal (chana, toor, urad, moong, masoor), ghee, jaggery, cardamom.
Origin Story+
A dish of Rajput soldiers — baatis could be buried in hot sand to bake while the army moved, then dug up days later, doused in ghee, and eaten with a quickly-made dal. The food of long marches and longer sieges.

LDinner
Rajasthan

Laal Maas

A fiery mutton curry stained crimson by Mathania red chillies — a royal Rajput delicacy of the hunting camps.

Ingredients+
Goat meat, Mathania chilli, garlic, yogurt, mustard oil, smoked clove, garam masala.
Origin Story+
Cooked in the hunting camps of the Rathore kings of Marwar — game meat, chilli and yogurt thrown into a single pot over a wood fire, the smoke from the clove pinch finishing the dish.

GDessert
Rajasthan

Ghevar

A honeycomb-disc of fried batter soaked in saffron syrup and crowned with rabri — the monsoon sweet of Jaipur.

Ingredients+
Maida, ghee, milk, sugar, saffron, cardamom, rabri, slivered almond, silver leaf.
Origin Story+
A festival sweet of Teej and Raksha Bandhan — Jaipur's halwais set up giant ghee cauldrons by mid-July and ladle the batter in spirals, the disc forming around the spoon as it fries.
