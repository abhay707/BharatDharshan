✦About the Festival✦

The Festival of Lights

Diwali — from the Sanskrit Deepavali, "a row of lamps" — is the brightest festival on the Hindu calendar. For five autumn nights, courtyards, balconies and temple plinths from Kanyakumari to Kashmir flicker with thousands of clay diyas, marking the return of Rama to Ayodhya after fourteen years of exile and the triumph of righteousness over the demon king Ravana.

It is a festival of renewal: homes are scrubbed and whitewashed, account books are closed and reopened, and rangolis bloom on doorsteps in coloured rice and marigold petals. Lakshmi, the goddess of abundance, is invoked to step over the threshold and bless the year ahead.

Beyond its Hindu origins, Diwali is observed across Jain, Sikh and Buddhist traditions — each carrying a different memory of liberation, of escape from darkness into knowing. The festival is the quiet centre of India's lunar autumn, when fields rest after the kharif harvest and families gather across distances they would not cross any other month.

How It Is Celebrated

Days before the festival, homes are deep-cleaned and adorned with strings of marigold and mango leaf torans. Doorsteps blossom with rangoli — geometric mandalas drawn in coloured powder, rice flour and crushed petals — and clay diyas are filled with mustard oil and a twisted cotton wick.

On the main night of Lakshmi Puja, families gather in their cleanest white-and-gold finery to perform a brief aarti before a small altar of new currency notes, gold coins, and an idol of the goddess. Sweets — kaju katli, motichoor laddoo, gulab jamun — are exchanged in lacquered tin boxes between neighbours and colleagues. After dinner, terraces fill with sparklers, anaars and aerial chakras; the night sky unbroken by silence.

Diwali marks the victory of light over darkness, knowledge over ignorance, and good over evil — the moment the inner lamp is lit.

✦Key Rituals & Traditions✦

Sacred Rituals of Diwali

Day OneDhanteras

Shopping for Gold & Utensils

The festival opens with the worship of Dhanvantari, physician of the gods. Households buy a small piece of gold, silver or new brass — an act believed to invite Lakshmi's prosperity into the year. Markets stay open long past midnight.

Day TwoNaraka Chaturdashi

Oil Bath at Dawn

Before sunrise, every member of the family receives an abhyanga — a ritual oil massage with sesame and a bath with ubtan. It commemorates Krishna's slaying of the demon Narakasura and washes away a year's accumulated dust of the soul.

Day ThreeLakshmi Puja

The Main Evening of Diwali

The new moon night. Diyas are lit in every doorway and window so the goddess will not lose her way. A short puja invokes Lakshmi alongside Ganesha and Saraswati; account books are sanctified, sweets distributed, and the night sky lit with crackers and aerial flowers.

Day FourGovardhan Puja

Offerings to Lord Krishna

A miniature mountain of food — the annakut, "mound of grain" — is built in temples in memory of the Govardhan hill Krishna lifted on his little finger to shelter the cowherds of Vrindavan from Indra's wrath. The offerings are then served to all comers.

Day FiveBhai Dooj

Brothers & Sisters

Sisters mark their brothers' foreheads with a tilak of vermilion and rice and feed them by hand; brothers in turn vow protection and bring gifts. The festival closes on the bond that crossed Yama, lord of death, when his sister Yami invited him home for a meal.

✦Your Complete Festival Guide✦

Everything You Need to Know

What to Wear

Silks & Festive Hues

Opt for traditional Indian attire — silk sarees, lehengas or sherwanis in red, gold and green. Avoid black and pure white at puja gatherings; both are considered inauspicious for Lakshmi's night.

What to Eat

The Mithai Box

Don't miss gulab jamun, kaju katli and warm rice kheer. For savoury counterpoint, try chakli, chivda and crisp shankarpali. Most homes will press a box on you as you leave — accept it.

What to Carry

Sweets & Small Gifts

A box of mithai, dry fruits in a brass dabba, or a small silver coin make perfect host gifts. Wrap in red or gold paper; avoid black ribbon and never gift in odd numbers below seven.

Photography

Diyas at Dusk

The best shots happen at the blue hour — diyas already lit, sky still glowing. Use portrait mode at f/1.8 for bokeh; long-exposure on a small tripod captures sparkler trails beautifully.

Safety

Crackers & Crowds

Keep a bucket of water and sand near firework displays. Loose dupattas, synthetic clothes and long open hair are hazards. Be mindful of asthmatic neighbours and pets — buy "green" crackers where available.

Transport

Move Before the Rush

Cities slow to a halt by Lakshmi Puja evening; book cabs or trains a week in advance. Domestic airfare doubles the week of Diwali — flights from Delhi, Mumbai and Bengaluru fill first.

Etiquette

Shoes, Sweets & Smiles

Always remove shoes before entering homes for puja. Accept sweets graciously — refusing is considered rude. Greet elders with a slight bow; "Shubh Deepavali" works in any region.

Best Spots

Where Diwali Glows Brightest

Varanasi's ghats during Dev Deepawali, Jaipur's old city bazaars, Goa's Narakasura effigy parades, Amritsar's Golden Temple at dusk, and Kolkata's Kali Puja pandals — five faces of the same fire.
